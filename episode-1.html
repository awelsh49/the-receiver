The Night It Appeared

Brody Johnson is twenty-seven. He works late shifts at a distribution warehouse on the edge of Alder Reach, a small town in northern Ontario. Most people call it industrial. Brody calls it forgettable.

The town sits beneath a web of towers and lines that hum day and night. Microwave dishes. Power stations. Fiber hubs. Infrastructure people drive past without thinking about. It’s not a place you visit. It’s a place you drive through or live in. Quiet. Cold. Unremarkable.

On a Thursday in early October, Brody stepped onto his back deck as the rain poured down in buckets. He liked the rain and was capturing a video of it illuminated by a street lamp.

That’s when he saw it.

In the phone’s screen, something appeared in the frame that wasn’t in the street.

At first it was a dark smudge, barely noticable against the blackness of the night. But then its form became solid and through the screen it was visibly in front of him. He pushed the phone aside to see if it was actually there, but there was nothing to see. It only existed when he looked through his phone. It looked like a piece of machinery. Perhaps a lamp or old instrument. Weathered metal and glass.

He moved the phone and the object stayed in place. Not in the world. But certainly in the frame.

Brody doesn’t know what it is. But it feels important. Then on his screen appears an input field. But an input to where? What should he type? Is it looking for a response?
